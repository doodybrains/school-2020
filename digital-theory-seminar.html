technical objects are the results of a series of convergences of functions, i.e. earlier engines had cooling fins located by the valves on the outside of the structure, they served only the purpose of cooling down the machine where it was subject to high pressure and heat, in 1950s engine the cooling fins also had the mechanical function of protecting the cylinder head of the engine from deterioration,

it is the convergence of these two "needs" of the machine that constitutes its moving away from abstraction and into concretization, where a compromise is made between parts and functions within the machine, i.e. best case scenario and best case scenario for rigidity; it is the progressive reduction of this margin between the functions of plurivalent structures that defines the progress of a technical object

what happens when machines begin to imitate other machines? he wittles down the concept of digital machines to a limited set of criteria, one which had not yet been developed; computers were not yet solidified or ubiquitous.

turing invents what we theoretically know as a computer, at the time calling it a univeral state machine, in which it is able to imitate all other machines, if a discrete state machine can run a single program, the universal machine can be programmed to mimic any other machine; any machine can perform the instructions of any other machine

turing posits that by the end of the 20th century univeral machines could imitate humans, given enough storage and speed.
