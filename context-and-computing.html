Myth: The Genius Founder

What strikes me is that none of the men who have "innovated" platforms from their "genius" have ever risen to the occasion of anything regarding their users’ collective concerns. Perhaps this is because there are no collective concerns? At least when Zuckerberg did those hearings it always seemed like he was on the defense, yes and no answers to questions, not necessarily taking accountability for how his platform might have harmful effects. These "genius founders", like Steve Jobs, have simply been boosted by their ability to work with computers, the money that followed, and now governments and institutions of great power. They hold on to their platforms tightly as more and more people join them. Perhaps they believe their power to be justified since we all somewhat willingly join only to realize that everything that we do belongs in the end to them.

I am thinking about genius founders as the people who have created something which became much bigger than them and was easily put into the hands of people on a hyper-scale. Often these founders are finding problems that their innovations can solve after the fact. For example, Mark Zuckerberg. I guess there's also such a thing as a "genius organizer" which Tiana Reid points out when discussing how Astra Taylor taps into this "singular dude" myth. Taylor recounts a quote from Martin Luther King: " “No, movements need a leader.” They go on to discuss how this is the antithesis to Ella Baker's style of organizing in which movements are leaderless and the core mission of the movement is energized by the collective.

I supposed I'm tripped up a little on some of the thinking around these readings because I keep flip-flopping between platforms, where so much power lies, and movements. I guess I'm wondering do these platforms need movements to move against them. Perhaps movements work best when they are by people for people, regardless of offline or online injustice. I suppose what I'm saying is that movements might need Ella Baker style organizing and platforms might need Mary Parker Follett style organizing. Or vice versa? Or both?

A power-with platform like Facebook or Instagram sounds interesting. It occurs to me that this class is performing teacher-student “joint-thinking” and perhaps that is part of what makes it feel so different from other ITP classes where the motivation is to move fast, solve quickly, and go for individual ideas. Even collaboration can feel individual because unless there is a lot of work put into dissolving the imbalance of power the process comes down to a power-over instead of a power-with way of working.

Myth: Everyone should be here: its better to be connected.

What is Mark Zuckerberg’s impetus for connecting everyone in the world? My hunch is that it’s to ensure some kind of panoptic eye so that he can keep the data brokerages, governments, and whatever other institutions he partners with happy. It was interesting to me how he wrote that the printing press and the television simply made communication more efficient. Not sure I agree here. I think they changed the way communication travels, what it looks like, what is or isn’t communicated but I would argue that communication between people can be just as efficient without these things.

Facebook most definitely changed the way we communicate. Maybe not Facebook but the Internet, email, chat. Facebook made a form of internet communication easy while exploiting the innate human need for social connection. Why does Mark Zuckerberg’s want a more open culture? Reminds me of the right to opacity, the right to privacy. He doesn’t really acknowledge these perhaps because he assumes that everything you do should be exposed which is an ideology that is rooted in violence.

Let us not forget that Facebook was originally called FaceSmash a place for to rate women Harvard students based on their hotness.

It’s interesting to think about Facebook as one big organization failing from the tyranny of structurelessness. Its tyranny is that there is a very large informal group of people (users) with a very clear power dynamic and a single leader. In this sense Facebook is an "elitist" group because only a few have immense power over many. This fact in itself should be enough to put people up in arms. The problem being that no one using Facebook has insight into its structure, no one has the power to change it. Is the problem then that we don’t even know what we want because we never knew we wanted something like Facebook in the first place?

I think if everyone needs to be connected to everyone else then there should be clearly defined and structured groups for doing so but these groups needs to be created by users themselves not by the few in power. This in the end would look like different kinds of connections (protocols?) between different people.

Myth: The Internet has no place, on the internet you have no face

John Perry Barlow, a cattle rancher, a cyberlibertarian political activist and lyricist for the Grateful Dead, wrote A Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace in 1996 which really did its job in perpetuating this myth. I remember, in 2010, having never read this, feeling something close to this idea, being online and truly feeling like I was in a place that had no physical location, truly feeling like who I was in the physical world need not be who I was online. However, I don't agree with these ideas, mostly I don't agree with the format of these ideas, that they are in a kind of declaration, that it is declarative, that somehow JPB's needs are all people's needs, that the kind of independence he is seeking is the same independence all people online should seek. I do think there is power in the fact that people can gather in a non-physical online space where their identities can be fluid but that is not the same thing as saying that when you are online, you do not have a body, that the powers that be don't also affect how you get online, what you see online, how the systems that created the Internet are also the systems that created the military, how institutionalized racism precedes the material output of these technologies and so are inherently baked in. This is where JPB makes sweeping oversights, where he assumes that there is such a thing as a neutral, apolitical space. I would say that the most obvious argument against this myth is that removing human bodies from any equation where humans are participating is a political act with very serious consequences.



By removing bodies we remove the possibility of making material in the first place. In writing this response I think it makes the most sense to work backwards from Johanna Drucker's Performative Materialist and Theoretical Approaches to Interface. An interesting question here might be...what would the declaration of independence of cyberspace be if it were framed by performative materiality? Perhaps the idea of digital flux can already be read into JPB's statement when he says "transactions, relationships, and thought itself, arrayed like a standing wave in the web of our communications." But then immediately after he writes "...but it is no where bodies live." which is where I would jump back to Blanchette's distributed materiality which Drucker describes as not only being rooted in the acknowledgement of all elements in a system being material themselves but also "locked into relations with each other that are governed by their material design" I think this complicates JPB's concept of collective self-governance and the "we". Its kind of a "tyranny of structureless" situation. But also, from this distributed materialist point of view, the structures which allow for "transactions" are themselves material, and therefore the structures are acting and are political.



Trevor Paglen touches on this in his essay Experimental Geography: From Cultural Production to the Production of Space noting "The spaces humans produce, in turn, set powerful constraints upon subsequent activity." My questions now are was what Walter Benjamin was positing also a kind of performative materiality? The idea that what is produced also itself produces a space. I'm reminded if an essay about facial recognition by Os Keyes in which they write "Because all technology that implicates gender, alters it; more generally, all technology that measures a thing alters it simply by measuring it." The mere act of measuring something, designing something, studying something, all alter the thing. The article ‘Speculative infrastructures’ at the urban margins notes "While practices of remaking at the margins create means of access, they also simultaneously highlight for us the very biased politics of planning and design". There is no doubt here that Barlow's idea of cyberspace was a design in itself, meant to exist on top of pre-existing material structures, both of which, the idea, and the material structures, work together to become political.
